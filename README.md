# Advection-Equation-Inverse-Problem-PINN-Experiment
These two notebooks compare the high-frequency ((k=8)) and low-frequency ((k=2)) advection inverse PINN problems. The PDE structure is the same in both experiments, and the main difference lies in the frequency (k) appearing in the exact solution.

The experimental results show that the low-frequency case ((k=2)) is significantly easier to train. Although the standard PINN may still fail to correctly identify (c), adding Fourier features allows the PINN to represent the oscillatory solution more effectively and train the unknown wave speed (c) close to its true value. For example, the Fourier-feature PINN, hard-periodic Fourier PINN, faster-(c) training, loss curriculum, and adaptive weighting methods all produce relatively accurate estimates of (c) and low prediction errors in the (k=2) case.

In contrast, the original high-frequency case ((k=8)) is much more challenging. When (k) is large, even a small error in (c) can lead to a large phase shift. As a result, the loss landscape of the high-frequency inverse problem is more likely to contain misleading or incorrect basins. The experiments show that standard joint PINN training, data-guided pretraining, alternating least squares, loss curriculum, adaptive weighting, and the (c)-visible transport-coordinate PINN still cannot reliably recover the correct value of (c) when starting from a poor initialization of (c).

However, the high-frequency experiments also show that the neural network is not completely unable to learn the solution. If (c) is initialized close to the true value, and if the number of training points and training iterations is increased appropriately, the high-frequency prediction results can also improve significantly. This suggests that the main difficulty is not that the PDE residual is incorrectly formulated, nor that the neural network is fundamentally incapable of representing the high-frequency solution. Instead, the key difficulty is that ordinary joint PINN training struggles to move (c) from an incorrect basin to the correct basin automatically.

Therefore, these two experiments jointly support the following conclusion: the unknown parameter (c) is theoretically identifiable, but vanilla PINN-style joint optimization is not reliable for high-frequency pure advection inverse problems. In the low-frequency case, Fourier features are already sufficient to substantially improve both function representation and parameter identification. In the high-frequency case, however, general-purpose PINN techniques are usually not enough. Stronger inverse-specific strategies are needed, such as characteristic-based or grid-based initialization, which first place (c) in the correct basin before applying PINN fine-tuning.
